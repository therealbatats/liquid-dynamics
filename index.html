What is Liquid Dynamics?

The Insight

Liquids are not passive substrates. Ionic concentration gradients governed by the Nernst-Planck equations perform nonlinear transformations on signals.

The Theorem

Energy-Computation Decoupling: ∂E/∂A = 0, ∂P/∂V = 0. Increasing computational power costs no additional energy.

The Proof

18 simulations from XOR gates to online learning, validating every prediction.

The Physics

Nernst-Planck Equation
J_i = −D_i∇c_i − (z_i·F·D_i / R·T)·c_i·∇φ
Governs ionic flux through the electrolyte. The first term represents diffusion, the second represents electromigration under the potential gradient. This coupled system enables ion-mediated signal transformation.
Continuity Equation
∂c_i/∂t + ∇·J_i = 0
Enforces conservation of ionic mass. As ions drift and diffuse, their concentration changes dynamically, creating the temporal dynamics essential for computation.
Poisson Equation
∇²φ = −(F/ε₀εᵣ)·Σ z_i·c_i
Couples the potential field to charge distribution. The local potential determines electromigration rates, creating feedback loops that enable nonlinear transformations of input signals.

The Physical Device

The Liquid Dynamics prototype is a compact $173 hardware system demonstrating ionic computation in real-world conditions. The device integrates a peristaltic pump for fluid control, dual Ag/AgCl electrodes for electrochemical signaling, and an Arduino-based PID controller for precise temperature and ionic concentration management.

• Peristaltic Pump: 0.1 − 10 mL/min variable flow
• Electrodes: Ag/AgCl with teflon-insulation
• Control: Arduino Uno + custom firmware (PID loop 100 Hz)
• Chamber: 2 mL polycarbonate with optical readout ports
• Power: 5V USB, <100 mA draw

Complete device design documentation, CAD files, and firmware are available in the Device Design repository.

Research Paper

Liquids as Active Computational Media: A Unified Framework for Computing with Ions at the Liquid-Electrode Interface

Abstract (Opening): We present a complete theoretical and experimental framework for performing computation using ionic concentration gradients in electrolyte solutions. By leveraging the Nernst-Planck equations governing ion transport, we demonstrate that a liquid-electrode system can execute arbitrary logic operations, perform analog signal processing, and implement online learning algorithms. Crucially, we prove that computational throughput decouples from energy consumption—a fundamental advantage over silicon.

The paper includes:

  • Mathematical proofs of energy-computation decoupling
  • 18 fully-validated simulation studies
  • Experimental characterization of the $173 prototype device
  • Comparison to biological neural systems and silicon computing
  • Scaling laws and information-theoretic limits
